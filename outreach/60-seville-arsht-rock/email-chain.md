Hi Jen,

I'm Arnaud, founder of CommunityFix (communityfix.org) — an open knowledge base that documents real-world solutions to urban and climate challenges so other cities can replicate what works.

We've just published a case study on Seville's proMETEO pilot: the naming and categorisation system for extreme heat events that produced "Zoe," the world's first officially named heat wave. Given Arsht-Rock's central role in that initiative alongside the City of Seville, AEMET, Universidad de Sevilla, and Universidad Pablo de Olavide, I wanted to reach out directly.

Two things I'd love your input on:

1. **Accuracy check** — would you or someone on your team be willing to take a quick look and flag anything that needs correcting or updating?
2. **Enrichment** — if there are metrics, lessons learned, or resources from your side that would make the case study more useful to cities considering a similar approach, we'd be glad to add them (with full attribution).

The goal is to make it as useful as possible for climate resilience practitioners elsewhere. There's no commercial angle — CommunityFix is a public resource.

The idea behind CommunityFix is simple: by documenting what was actually tried — and what came of it, costs and pitfalls included — other cities, regions and countries can avoid the same mistakes and go straight to the solutions that work. It's a small but real contribution to a better world: turning one community's hard-won experience into knowledge everyone can build on.

Happy to share the direct link to the case study if useful.

Thanks for the work Arsht-Rock is doing on this front — naming heat waves the way we name hurricanes feels like one of those deceptively simple ideas that can genuinely shift public response.

Best,  
Arnaud Gissinger, Founder — CommunityFix · communityfix.org
